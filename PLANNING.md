# Transfusion Medicine — One-Day Crash Course
## Phase 1 Planning Package

> Built for **Eric** — a clinical pathology resident, Henry Ford Pathology Residency.
> Author/educator: academic pathologist & transfusion medicine educator.

This document is the planning and content blueprint that the dashboard (`index.html`) is built from. It is intentionally written so the dashboard could be rebuilt or extended without guessing.

---

## A. Learner Profile

| Dimension | Assumption |
|---|---|
| **Audience** | Medical students, single-day rotation in Transfusion Medicine (TM) |
| **Baseline knowledge** | Limited prior exposure to blood banking, coagulation, and apheresis |
| **Existing strengths** | Solid clinical medicine framework; comfortable with anemia, bleeding, thrombosis, sepsis, and general hospital workflow |
| **Cognitive goal** | Understand *what TM does in real practice* and *how to reason through common scenarios safely* — not to memorize a reference manual |
| **Failure mode to avoid** | Drowning novices in serology/factor-cascade minutiae before they have a clinical scaffold |
| **Design implication** | Clinical language first → lab framework second → technical detail flagged as optional/"advanced pearl" |

**Teaching stance:** scaffold simple → complex, explain every abbreviation on first use, prioritize pattern recognition, and always connect the lab back to a bedside decision.

## B. Learning Objectives (high-yield, by pillar)

### Pillar 1 — Blood Components & Blood Bank
By the end of the day, the learner can:
1. Explain what the blood bank does and why "type & screen" precedes most transfusions.
2. State what each product (pRBC, platelets, plasma, cryoprecipitate) contains, its core indication, and when *not* to use it.
3. Apply ABO/Rh compatibility logic and identify the universal RBC donor (O) and universal plasma donor (AB).
4. Recognize when emergency-release (O-negative) blood is appropriate.
5. Choose between standard and modified products (irradiated, leukoreduced, CMV-safe, washed) for high-risk patients.
6. Recognize the major transfusion reaction buckets and perform the universal first step: **stop the transfusion**.

### Pillar 2 — Coagulation Testing & Interpretation *(the centerpiece)*
1. Describe a reproducible mental model: *bleeding vs thrombosis*, *inherited vs acquired*, *screening vs specialized*.
2. Interpret PT/INR and aPTT and localize a defect to the extrinsic, intrinsic, or common pathway.
3. Work up an isolated prolonged PT, isolated prolonged aPTT, or both prolonged.
4. Use a **mixing study** to separate factor **deficiency** from **inhibitor**.
5. Recognize the classic patterns: DIC, liver disease, vitamin K deficiency/warfarin, heparin, hemophilia, vWD, and lupus anticoagulant.
6. Distinguish platelet **count** from platelet **function**, and know what D-dimer and fibrinogen add.

### Pillar 3 — Apheresis Medicine
1. Define therapeutic vs donor apheresis and explain the shared principle: *selectively remove or exchange one blood component.*
2. Match the core modalities (plasma exchange, red cell exchange, cytapheresis, HPC collection) to the problem each solves.
3. List the high-yield indications a student should recognize on consult: **TTP**, sickle cell (stroke/ACS), hyperviscosity, hyperleukocytosis, and select neuro-immune disease.
4. Explain replacement-fluid choice (albumin vs plasma) and why TTP uses plasma.
5. Recognize the common complications: citrate/hypocalcemia, hypotension, and line issues.

## D. Core Content Map (must know / should recognize / advanced-optional)

### Pillar 1 — Blood Bank
- **Must know:** ABO/Rh basics; type & screen vs crossmatch; the four core products + indications; "stop the transfusion" as the universal first move; O-neg emergency release.
- **Should recognize:** product modifications (irradiated/leukoreduced/CMV-safe/washed) and who needs them; TACO vs TRALI; massive transfusion 1:1:1 concept.
- **Advanced / optional:** antibody identification panels, antigen-negative unit sourcing, specific minor antigen systems (Kell/Duffy/Kidd) beyond naming, HLA platelet matching detail.

### Pillar 2 — Coagulation
- **Must know:** what PT and aPTT measure; the deficiency-vs-inhibitor concept; mixing study logic; DIC / liver / vitamin K / heparin patterns; platelet count vs function.
- **Should recognize:** factor assays and when they are sent; lupus anticoagulant paradox (long aPTT, clinically prothrombotic); D-dimer and fibrinogen roles; specimen-quality pitfalls (clotted/hemolyzed/underfilled tube).
- **Advanced / optional:** specific factor half-lives, dRVVT mechanics, thromboelastography, individual thrombophilia panels, factor inhibitor titration (Bethesda units).

### Pillar 3 — Apheresis
- **Must know:** the four core modalities and the problem each solves; TTP = plasma exchange is lifesaving; albumin vs plasma replacement; citrate/hypocalcemia.
- **Should recognize:** ASFA category concept (I–IV); sickle red cell exchange indications; hyperviscosity and hyperleukocytosis; line/access realities.
- **Advanced / optional:** detailed ASFA category lists, photopheresis, LDL apheresis, granulocyte collection logistics, CD34+ mobilization pharmacology specifics.

## E. Clinical-Language-First Translation

Every technical concept in the dashboard is framed with three lenses so novices anchor the lab to the bedside:

- **Why it matters** — the clinical stake (bleeding, clotting, hemolysis, mortality).
- **When it comes up** — the real situation that triggers it (trauma bay, pre-op, oncology floor, ICU consult).
- **What to do next** — the interpretation or action expected of the trainee.

Example (aPTT): *Why it matters* — a long aPTT can mean a bleeding risk (factor deficiency) **or** a clotting risk (lupus anticoagulant); the two are opposite. *When it comes up* — pre-op "abnormal coags," a bleeding patient, or an incidental result. *What to do next* — confirm it's not a heparin/specimen artifact, then order a **mixing study** to split deficiency from inhibitor.

## F. Resource-Gathering Plan (source *types* — no fabricated citations)

The dashboard content is synthesized from standard, well-established teaching knowledge. For deeper verification and future updates, consult these **real, named source types** (specific editions/links to be added by the educator; none are invented here):

- **AABB** — *Technical Manual* and *Standards for Blood Banks and Transfusion Services* (component specs, compatibility, reactions).
- **AABB / ICCBBA / FDA Circular of Information** for the Use of Human Blood and Blood Components (authoritative product descriptions).
- **ASFA** — *Guidelines on the Use of Therapeutic Apheresis* (the categorized indication list, published in the *Journal of Clinical Apheresis*).
- **ASH** (American Society of Hematology) education program and *Blood* reviews — coagulation, TTP, DIC, hemophilia.
- **CAP** (College of American Pathologists) resources for laboratory interpretation and specimen quality.
- **ISTH** (International Society on Thrombosis and Haemostasis) — DIC scoring, lupus anticoagulant testing guidance.
- **British Society for Haematology (BSH) guidelines** — pragmatic transfusion and coagulation guidance.
- Standard textbooks: *Rossi's Principles of Transfusion Medicine*; a clinical hemostasis/thrombosis reference; a general hematology text.
- Reputable point-of-care references (e.g., UpToDate-style summaries) for bedside framing.

> Labeled honestly: the above are **source types to consult**, named accurately. The dashboard UI deliberately contains **no fabricated citations or DOIs**; a "Further reading" note points learners to these named bodies.

### 2. Content Blueprint (per pillar)
Each pillar section contains: a short orientation strip → teaching cards / comparison grid / algorithm → an embedded case → an embedded mini-quiz → pearls & pitfalls accordion. Long-form teaching is semantic HTML; repetitive structured content (questions, cases, glossary, product specs) is data-driven in JS so it is trivial to extend.

### 3. Module / Component List
Hero/orientation • 3-pillar overview cards • blood product cards • compatibility logic panel • product comparison grid • transfusion-reaction recognition cards • coagulation framework • PT/aPTT algorithm • pattern cards (DIC/liver/vit K/heparin/hemophilia/vWD/LA) • deficiency-vs-inhibitor explainer • apheresis modality cards • consult-indication cards • replacement-fluid panel • risk panel • Case Lab (progressive disclosure) • Quiz Center (per-pillar + mixed, scored) • searchable glossary • final recap + rounds pearls • theme toggle • progress indicator • animated hematology background (canvas, reduced-motion aware).

### 4. Recommended Teaching Flow for the Day
1. **Orient (5 min):** Start Here — set expectations, the 3-pillar mental map.
2. **Anchor on products (45 min):** Blood Bank cards → comparison grid → reactions → quiz. This is concrete and motivating.
3. **Build the framework (60 min):** Coagulation — teach the *thinking* before tests; walk the PT/aPTT algorithm; do the embedded cases live; quiz.
4. **Show the consult service (40 min):** Apheresis — frame as "problems we get paged about," run TTP and sickle cases.
5. **Integrate (30 min):** Case Lab — cross-pillar reasoning with "what next?" steps.
6. **Self-test (20 min):** Quiz Center mixed review; review misses together.
7. **Consolidate (10 min):** Recap take-home points + rounds pearls; assign the glossary as a quick-reference to keep.
